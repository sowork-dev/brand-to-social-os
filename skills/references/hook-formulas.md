# LinkedIn Hook Formulas

Reference document for Skill 06 -- LinkedIn Operator Pack.
Use this as a lookup table when selecting a hook format for a post.

---

## What a hook must do

The hook is the first line of a LinkedIn post. It is the only line visible in the feed
before the reader clicks 'See more'. It has one job: make the reader click 'See more'.

A hook fails if it is:
- Generic (could apply to any post in the category)
- About the company or team instead of the reader's problem
- Positive and celebratory in tone (triggers skip response)
- A complete thought (no reason to continue reading)

A hook works if it creates:
- A tension the reader wants resolved
- A specific claim the reader wants verified
- A question the reader is already asking
- A scene the reader wants to follow

---

## Banned openers

Never start a post with any of the following. These openers reliably reduce engagement:

| Banned opener | Why it fails |
|---------------|--------------|
| I am excited to... | Corporate PR tone. Signals self-promotion. |
| We are proud to... | About the company, not the reader. |
| Thrilled to share... | Signals announcement. Reader skips. |
| Happy to announce... | Same as above. |
| Delighted to... | Formal and distant. No tension. |
| Just wanted to say... | Filler. No substance. |
| I (alone, no further context) | Triggers self-promo detection in reader's brain. |
| We (alone, no further context) | Company-centric. Reader does not care yet. |
| Today I learned... | Overused to the point of parody. |
| Big news!... | Always overpromises. Rarely delivers. |

## The 6 hook formulas

### Formula 1 -- Contrarian claim

**Structure:** [Widely believed thing] is wrong. Here is why.

**When to use:** Point-of-view pillar posts. When the brand has a genuine strategic
contrast to the category norm.

**Examples:**
- Most content calendars are why your content is failing.
- Brand positioning is not about being different. It is about being specific.
- The biggest LinkedIn mistake is not posting too little. It is posting without a point of view.

**Taiwan brand adaptation:**
- Most Taiwan brands do not have a positioning problem. They have a translation problem.
- Localisation is not the same as positioning. Changing the language is not enough.

**Scoring:** High tension, high shareability. Use once per week maximum. If overused,
the brand becomes known for contrarianism, not expertise.

---

### Formula 2 -- Numbered insight

**Structure:** [N] [things/reasons/ways] [specific audience] [never tells you / gets wrong / should know] about [specific topic]

**When to use:** Education pillar posts. Works for any topic where the brand has
compiled real observations from working with clients or researching the market.

**Examples:**
- 4 things your audience wants from LinkedIn content that most brands never give them.
- 3 reasons Taiwan brands plateau at 2,000 followers when entering global markets.
- 5 signals that your brand positioning is too generic (and how to fix each one).

**Taiwan brand adaptation:**
- 6 mistakes Taiwan B2B brands make in their first 90 days of English LinkedIn content.
- 3 things US buyers notice immediately when a Taiwan brand enters their market.

**Scoring:** Reliable format. Medium-high engagement. Easy to produce. Risk: overuse
makes the feed feel like a listicle blog. Balance with other formats.

---

### Formula 3 -- Before / after

**Structure:** From [specific bad state] to [specific good state] in [timeframe or condition change].

**When to use:** Proof pillar posts. Requires real before/after data. Do not use
with invented or approximated numbers.

**Examples:**
- From 3 DMs a month to 40 qualified conversations -- same audience, different approach.
- From zero English posts to a consistent weekly system in 6 weeks.
- From broad brand statement to a one-sentence position that makes buyers say 'that is exactly us'.

**Taiwan brand adaptation:**
- From posting in Chinese to an English-speaking audience to owning a clear market position in 90 days.
- From reacting to industry news to publishing a strategic POV that drives inbound.

**Scoring:** Highest trust signal when numbers are real and specific. Very low trust signal
when numbers are vague or obviously inflated.

---

### Formula 4 -- Specific observation

**Structure:** [Precise, non-obvious claim based on real observation or data]

**When to use:** Education and POV posts. Works when the brand has a genuine insight
from real work -- not from reading articles.

**Examples:**
- Every high-performing LinkedIn post I studied in the past 90 days had one thing in common.
- The most-shared Taiwan brand content in US markets is not product content. It is opinion content.
- Brands that post at 7 AM EST on Tuesday and Thursday consistently outperform weekend posters.

**Taiwan brand adaptation:**
- Every Taiwan brand I have worked with that successfully entered a Western market did one counterintuitive thing first.
- The gap between Taiwan brand positioning and Western buyer expectations is almost always in the same place.

**Scoring:** Requires genuine insight. Cannot be faked. Reader will test the claim against their
own experience. High reward when the observation is novel, high damage when it is trivially obvious.

---

### Formula 5 -- Direct question

**Structure:** [Question that creates cognitive dissonance or challenges an assumption]

**When to use:** POV and engagement-focused posts. The question should be one the
reader has not answered for themselves yet.

**Examples:**
- Why do brands with 50,000 followers get fewer leads than accounts with 2,000?
- What would happen if you stopped posting for 30 days -- would anyone notice?
- When was the last time a piece of your content changed how someone thought about something?

**Taiwan brand adaptation:**
- Why do most Taiwan brands have better products than their Western competitors and weaker positioning?
- What does a US buyer see when they land on your LinkedIn profile for the first time?

**Scoring:** Moderate engagement, high comment-pull. Works well for discussion and building
thought leadership. Risk: rhetorical questions without answers in the post body disappoint.

---

### Formula 6 -- Story opener

**Structure:** [Scene-setting sentence that creates immediate tension or curiosity]

**When to use:** Behind-the-scenes and proof pillar posts. The scene must be real.
Invented stories are detected and damage trust.

**Examples:**
- Last Tuesday a founder sent me a LinkedIn post that had 12,000 impressions and zero leads.
- Six months ago we made a positioning decision that almost ended the company.
- A client emailed me at 6 AM on a Monday. The subject line was: it worked.

**Taiwan brand adaptation:**
- Last quarter a Taiwan hardware brand asked me to help them enter the US market. The first thing I noticed was not their product.
- A founder came to us with 9 years of market data and no idea how to turn it into a story.

**Scoring:** Highest retention format when the story delivers. Requires a real payoff.
If the story opener does not connect to a meaningful insight, readers feel manipulated.

## Hook selection guide

Use this table to select the right hook format based on content type and goal:

| Pillar | Business goal | Recommended formats |
|--------|--------------|---------------------|
| Education | Build trust, earn follow | Numbered insight, Specific observation |
| Point of view | Establish authority, drive debate | Contrarian claim, Direct question |
| Proof | Drive DMs, generate leads | Before/after, Story opener |
| Behind the scenes | Build loyalty, humanise | Story opener, Specific observation |
| Conversion | Drive action | Before/after, Direct question |

---

## Hook scoring rubric

Score each hook before delivering a post. Minimum to use: 8/10.

| Criterion | 10 | 7-9 | 5-6 | 1-4 |
|-----------|-----|-----|-----|-----|
| Stops the scroll | Irresistible, must read more | Interesting, would read | Generic but fine | Skippable |
| Specificity | Precise number, name, or situation | Some specificity | Vague | Completely abstract |
| Tension or curiosity | Strong open loop | Some tension | Mild interest | No tension |
| Voice alignment | Clearly this brand's voice | Mostly aligned | Neutral | Does not match |
| No banned openers | Zero banned words | -- | -- | Contains I, We, Excited etc |

---

## Hook testing protocol

Test hook effectiveness systematically:

1. Write two versions of the hook for each post
2. Score both on the rubric
3. Publish the higher-scoring version
4. After 48 hours: check engagement rate and note which hook format was used
5. Log in experiment backlog (skill 03) with: formula used, score, actual engagement rate

After 4-6 weeks of logging, patterns emerge: some formulas will outperform for your specific
audience. Weight toward those formulas while continuing to test the others monthly.

## Taiwan brand hook patterns

Based on observed performance from Taiwan brand accounts targeting English-speaking markets:

**Highest performing for Taiwan B2B brands entering global markets:**
- Formula 1 (Contrarian) performs best when the contrast is about market entry strategy, not product claims
- Formula 3 (Before/after) performs best when the 'after' state is quantified and the 'before' is painful
- Formula 6 (Story opener) performs best when the story involves a recognisable buyer situation

**Common hooks that fail for Taiwan brands:**
- Contrarian claims about obvious things ('Most companies ignore their customers' = too generic)
- Numbered lists about general marketing advice (too much competition in that space)
- Story openers that start with a meeting ('In a meeting last week...' = overused)
