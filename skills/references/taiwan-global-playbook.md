# Taiwan-to-Global Positioning Playbook

Tactical reference for Taiwan brands entering English-speaking markets.
Used by all 6 skills in the Brand to Social OS when the brand context is
'Taiwan brand targeting US, EU, or global English-speaking audiences'.

---

## The core tension

Taiwan brands entering global markets face a specific and consistent tension:

**Strong product, weak narrative.**

Most Taiwan brands have exceptional product quality, deep manufacturing expertise,
or proprietary data -- but have built their brand communication for domestic or
Asian markets. The shift to Western markets requires not just translation,
but a repositioning of how the brand explains its value.

The typical symptom: a Western buyer lands on the brand's LinkedIn or website
and thinks 'I can see this is a good product, but I do not understand why
I should choose this over what I already know.'

The problem is almost never product quality. It is narrative clarity.

## The four strategic transitions

Taiwan brands successfully entering global markets go through four transitions.
Each transition has a specific skill in this OS that handles it.

### Transition 1: From product-centric to problem-centric language

**Before (product-centric):** We provide high-quality data analysis solutions with
advanced AI capabilities and comprehensive market coverage.

**After (problem-centric):** Brands entering new markets are flying blind. They know
their product works. They do not know what Western buyers actually believe about
their category. We fix the second problem.

**Skill that handles this:** 01-brand-positioning (Strategic contrast section)

The strategic contrast must be written from the audience's problem, not the
brand's solution. The question to ask: what specific pain is the buyer experiencing
right now, today, that our brand exists to address?

### Transition 2: From category competitor to specific-alternative contrast

**Before (category competitor):** Better than traditional market research.

**After (specific alternative):** Most of our clients were using a combination of
Google Trends, Twitter monitoring, and quarterly agency reports to understand
Western buyer sentiment. That approach tells you what happened last quarter.
We tell you what is shifting this week.

**Skill that handles this:** 02-product-positioning (Alternative used today section)

Western buyers make purchase decisions based on specific alternatives, not category
comparisons. Name the real alternative. If the real alternative is 'reading English-
language articles manually', say that.

### Transition 3: From feature promotion to POV content

**Before (feature promotion):** Our platform supports 13 markets and 16 languages.

**After (POV content):** Most brands trying to enter the US market localise their
product first and build their positioning second. We believe it should be reversed.
Positioning determines what to build, not the other way around.

**Skill that handles this:** 04-content-pillar-design (Point of view pillar)

The POV pillar for Taiwan brands should be built around a specific belief about
the market that the brand can defend. Not marketing opinion -- strategic conviction
that comes from real experience in the market.

### Transition 4: From announcement-based to signal-based social publishing

**Before (announcement-based):** We are excited to share our new feature / award / partner.

**After (signal-based):** Last week we noticed that three of our clients in the US
market asked the same question about the same competitor within 4 days. Here is
what that pattern means.

**Skill that handles this:** 06-linkedin-operator-pack

Signal-based content is observation-driven. It works for Taiwan brands because
the brand's asset is knowledge -- about the market, about buyers, about patterns
that buyers cannot see themselves. Publish that knowledge, not your feature list.

## Market entry sequence

For Taiwan brands entering English-speaking markets from scratch:

### Phase 1 -- Foundation (weeks 1-4)

Objective: Establish a sharp brand position in English before publishing anything.

Actions:
- Run skill 01 (brand positioning) -- do not skip or rush this
- Run skill 02 (product positioning) -- produces the English-market pitch
- Do NOT start publishing yet

Common mistake in Phase 1: Starting to post in English before positioning is clear.
The first 90 days of content sets the brand's perceived positioning in the algorithm.
Early generic content is very hard to walk back.

### Phase 2 -- Infrastructure (weeks 3-6)

Objective: Build the content operating system and pillar structure.

Actions:
- Run skill 04 (content pillar design) -- all 5 pillars defined
- Run skill 03 (social operating system) -- weekly loop designed for actual capacity
- Decide on ONE primary channel for the first 90 days
- For most Taiwan B2B brands: LinkedIn first

Common mistake in Phase 2: Launching on multiple channels simultaneously.
One channel, done well, builds a referenceable audience. Three channels,
done poorly, create a fragmented presence that looks unprofessional.

### Phase 3 -- Publishing (weeks 5-16)

Objective: Build consistent presence and audience on the primary channel.

Actions:
- Run skill 06 (LinkedIn operator pack) -- weekly batch of 3-5 posts
- Run skill 05 (performance review) -- after each 2-week period
- Adjust pillar mix based on what resonates

Target metrics at week 12 (from scratch, consistent publishing):
- Follower growth: 50-150 net new followers per month (small account, English market)
- Engagement rate: 2-4% per post on education/POV content
- DMs or meaningful comments: 5-15 per week
- Profile visits from posts: 50-200 per week

### Phase 4 -- Optimisation (week 12 onward)

Objective: Double down on what works, eliminate what does not.

Actions:
- Monthly calibration via skill 05 (message-market fit check)
- Experiment with secondary channel if primary is stable
- Revisit brand positioning (skill 01) -- at least quarterly

## Language and voice guidance

### English writing for Taiwan brands targeting Western markets

**Active over passive:** Most Taiwan brand copy is written in passive voice.
Western B2B audiences read actively. Write every sentence in active voice.
Test: can you identify the subject doing the action? If not, rewrite.

Wrong: 'Advanced analytics are provided to help brands understand their market.'
Right: 'We map what Western buyers believe about your category, before you spend on distribution.'

**Specific over general:** The more specific the claim, the more credible.
Generality is the default mode of fear -- fear of saying something wrong,
fear of overpromising. Specificity is the default mode of confidence.

Wrong: 'We help brands achieve better market understanding.'
Right: 'We track social signal shifts in 13 Western markets, 16 languages, weekly.'

**Short sentences:** Long sentences are a signal of academic or bureaucratic writing.
Western B2B audiences read quickly and on mobile. Keep sentences under 20 words.
When in doubt, split the sentence.

**No hedging:** Words like 'solutions', 'leveraging', 'synergies', 'end-to-end', and
'comprehensive' are content that signals a lack of confidence in the actual claim.
Remove every hedge and rewrite with the direct claim it was avoiding.

### Cultural context for Western B2B audiences

Western B2B buyers (especially US and UK) respond to:
- Directness: say what you mean, mean what you say
- Specificity: numbers, names, examples
- Humility with conviction: acknowledge the hard parts, but have a clear POV
- Peer validation: who else has done this, what happened

Western B2B buyers are resistant to:
- Superlatives without evidence ('best', 'leading', 'world-class')
- Formality that creates distance
- Content that sounds like a press release
- Vague claims about technology or AI

## Validation checklist for Taiwan brands

Before running any skill for a Taiwan brand targeting global markets, confirm:

- [ ] Has the brand spoken directly to at least 5 potential Western buyers (not distributors, not resellers -- buyers)?
- [ ] Does the brand have at least 1 verifiable proof point from a Western market (not just Asia success stories)?
- [ ] Can the founder articulate in one sentence what Western buyers use today instead of this product?
- [ ] Is the strategic contrast specific to the Western market, not the Taiwan/Asian market context?
- [ ] Are the voice principles written in English, tested against English content?

If more than 2 boxes are unchecked, run skill 01 with a specific focus on collecting
Western market input before proceeding to any downstream skill.
