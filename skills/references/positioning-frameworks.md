## Framework 1 — April Dunford: Obviously Awesome Positioning

**Best for:** B2B SaaS, tech products, companies with a new or misunderstood category.
**Use when:** The product is technically strong but the market does not understand why it matters, or the company is being compared to the wrong competitors.

### The 5 components

| Component | Question to answer |
|---|---|
| Competitive alternatives | What would the customer do if this product did not exist? |
| Unique attributes | What does this product have that alternatives do not? |
| Value for customers | What value do those attributes enable for the customer? |
| Customer characteristics | Who cares most about that value? |
| Best-fit market frame | What market context makes the value obvious? |

### When NOT to use this framework
- Consumer brands where emotional resonance matters more than rational differentiation
- Very early stage companies with no product-market fit signal yet
- Companies that have not spoken to at least 10 customers about their buying decision

### Common mistakes with this framework
- Listing product features as unique attributes (features are table stakes; unique attributes are structural advantages)
- Choosing the competitive alternative incorrectly (the real alternative is what the customer does TODAY, not who you think your competitor is)
- Writing positioning for the product team instead of the buyer

## Framework 2 — Jobs to be Done (JTBD)

**Best for:** Products where the use case is clear but the messaging feels too feature-focused.
**Use when:** You want to understand and express WHY customers hire the product, not just what it does.

### The core structure

When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome].

Example: When I am preparing for a US market entry, I want to understand what American buyers actually believe about our category, so I can build messaging that does not sound like a translation.

### 4 forces that drive and block adoption

| Force | Direction | Question |
|---|---|---|
| Push | Toward change | What makes the customer dissatisfied with the current solution? |
| Pull | Toward your product | What about your product attracts them? |
| Anxiety | Against change | What are they worried about if they switch? |
| Habit | Against change | What familiar behaviour keeps them with the current solution? |

**How to use this in brand positioning:**
The Push and Anxiety forces are the most valuable for content. Push = core tension. Anxiety = objections to address.

### When NOT to use this framework
- When the job to be done is so obvious it does not need articulation (hammers, basic utilities)
- As a substitute for customer research — JTBD requires interviews to be accurate

### Common mistakes
- Writing the job at the feature level (save time on email) instead of the outcome level (maintain relationships without manual effort)
- Ignoring the habit force — most product failures are habit problems, not desire problems

## Framework 3 — Strategic Contrast (Used in this OS)

**Best for:** Any brand building thought leadership or content-first growth.
**Use when:** The company has a clear point of view about what is wrong in the market.

### Structure

Most of this market still does [old way].
We believe the better model is [new way].
Here is why: [mechanism that makes the new way better].

### Quality criteria for a strong contrast

| Criterion | Strong | Weak |
|---|---|---|
| Specificity | Names what the market actually does | Uses vague generalisations |
| Falsifiability | A competitor could disagree with it | No one could disagree with it |
| Mechanism | Explains why the new way is better | Just asserts it is better |
| Courage | Says something the company actually believes | Safe enough that it sounds like everyone else |

### Taiwan brand common failure
Most Taiwan brands write a contrast that is true but not bold. Example:

Weak: Most brands focus on features. We focus on the customer.
Strong: Most Taiwan brands entering the US treat messaging as a translation problem. We treat it as a category problem — and that is why their campaigns fail even when the product is good.

## Framework 4 — Message Hierarchy

**Best for:** Product positioning (skill 02), homepage copy, sales decks.
**Use when:** You have a positioning statement and need to build the full messaging architecture.

### Structure

| Level | Purpose | Format |
|---|---|---|
| Primary promise | The single most important claim | One sentence, fits the positioning template |
| Supporting claim 1 | Proves or expands the primary promise | One sentence + one proof point |
| Supporting claim 2 | Addresses the most common objection | One sentence + one proof point |
| Supporting claim 3 | Adds a dimension the primary promise does not cover | One sentence + one proof point |
| Proof point summary | Evidence for all of the above | 3-5 specific, verifiable items |

### Rule: each level must be falsifiable
If no competitor could claim the same thing, the claim is probably generic. Push until the claim is specific enough to be disputed.

## Framework 5 — Hook Frameworks for LinkedIn (Skill 06)

### Choose by post type

| Post type | Best hook framework | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Education (how-to, framework) | Numbered insight or specific observation | Creates expectation of concrete value |
| POV (opinion, contrast) | Contrarian or direct question | Creates cognitive dissonance that forces engagement |
| Proof (case study, result) | Before/after or story opener | Anchors the result to a specific real situation |
| Behind the scenes | Story opener | Creates scene and tension without overselling |
| Conversion | Specific observation + consequence | Connects the post to a real cost before the offer |

### Hook framework details

**Contrarian**
Structure: [Widely held belief] is [wrong / backwards / a trap].
Condition: You must be able to back this up in the body. An unsupported contrarian hook damages credibility.

**Numbered insight**
Structure: [N] [things / reasons / mistakes] [specific audience] [specific situation].
Rule: The number must be real. Do not start with a number and then write only 3 items.

**Before/after**
Structure: From [specific bad state] to [specific good state] in [timeframe or context].
Rule: Both states must be specific and real. Vague before/after sounds like advertising.

**Specific observation**
Structure: [Precise claim based on direct observation, data, or experience].
Best for: Founders and operators with real access to data others do not have.

**Direct question**
Structure: [Question that creates cognitive dissonance or names an uncomfortable truth].
Rule: The question must not have an obvious answer. If everyone already knows the answer, the hook fails.

**Story opener**
Structure: [Date or situation] [what happened] [tension implied].
Rule: The tension must be clear in the first line. If the scene has no conflict, it is not a story opener — it is a diary entry.

### Banned openers — never use these on LinkedIn

| Banned opener | Why |
|---|---|
| I am excited to... | Signals a press release or self-promotion, readers scroll past |
| We are proud to... | Same as above |
| Thrilled / Delighted / Happy to share | Same as above |
| I wanted to... | Weak agency, reads as apologetic |
| In today's world... | Meaningless framing |
| As a [job title]... | Self-introduction as an opener is never the hook |
| Did you know? | Overused, feels generic |

## Choosing between frameworks

| Situation | Recommended framework |
|---|---|
| Building brand from scratch, no messaging exists yet | Framework 1 (April Dunford) |
| Have positioning but copy feels too feature-focused | Framework 2 (JTBD) |
| Have positioning but content lacks a clear point of view | Framework 3 (Strategic contrast) |
| Building homepage or sales deck messaging | Framework 4 (Message hierarchy) |
| Writing LinkedIn posts from existing positioning | Framework 5 (Hook frameworks) |
| Repositioning against a new competitor | Framework 1 (run again with new competitive alternative) |
